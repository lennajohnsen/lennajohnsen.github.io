Refugee Resettlement in the United States

Effects of Trump-era policies on refugees from Muslim Majority Countries

. . .

Historically the United States has led the world in refugee admissions, resettling an estimated third-to-half of the world’s refugees since World War II. However the Trump administration has changed that. For the first time the U.S. has resettled fewer refugees than the rest of the world. Given President Trump’s discriminatory rhetoric towards members of the Islamic faith, this decrease in refugees appears to particularly disadvantage refugees from Muslim-majority countries.

The Process

Admission as a refugee is the most challenging way for someone to enter the United States. To be placed in the United States, a refugee must go through a 20 step vetting process that involves screening from the United Nations and multiple U.S. federal agencies. The process from application to resettlement takes at a minimum eighteen months, but can stretch up to three years.

The settlement process is largely controlled by the presidency. Since 1980 when the policy was adopted, every year in October the Executive Branch sets a cap or “ceiling” to set an upper-limit of refugees admitted into the United States in the next fiscal, which historically averaged 96,000. The actual number of refugees settled in the U.S. often falls short of the cap. In response to the Syrian Civil War and the growing refugee crisis across the globe, the Obama administration increased the refugee cap from 85,000 in FY 2016 to 110,000 in FY 2017.

One of the first policy decisions made by the Trump administration was to halt refugees settlement in the United States. On January 27, 2017, President Trump signed Executive Order 13769, commonly known as the Muslim Ban, which, in addition to barring entry from seven Muslim-majority nations, imposed a 120-day ban on refugees entering the U.S.. While the courts intervened to portions of the ban, in October, 2017 the administration passed new vetting policies that further slowed down resettlement processes and imposed a new 90-day ban on 11 countries, nice of whom are majority-Muslim. These actions resulted in a dramatic slowdown in refugee resettlement. Despite the 110,000 ceiling in 2017, the United States only resettled 53,716 refugees. In 2018, the Trump administration further reduced the cap to a nearly 40-year low of 45,000, and, over the course of the year, resettled less than half of that target (22,491). In 2019, the Trump Administration announced it would further reduce the cap to 30,000 refugees and, as of May 2019, is well short of reaching that limit.
